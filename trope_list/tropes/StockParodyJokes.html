Luigi: Mario, it's me Luigi. I just can't stop eating the mushrooms. Hahaha...It's such a funny joke. I'm such a very, very funny fucking guy! [angrily throws bag]
PSA: Mario mushroom jokes kills six million each year. Help the [sic] find the cure. Donate today.
— JonTron, The Weird World of PSAs

Many parody jokes are not very original but are commonplace nevertheless. They get routinely reused in skits, comics, and the like. Sometimes these parodies are Shallow Parodies.

Advertisement:

Any work that includes magical or otherwise fantastical elements will inevitably get parodies in which the work is set in a naturalistic world and they are due to mental illness or drug hallucinations. Many examples also relate to moments of Never Live It Down. If a parody uses some of the Black Comedy stock jokes (like drug use, apocalypses, etc), it's a Dark Parody.

Compare to Stock Epileptic Trees for common general theories, Stock Parodies for works that get parodied frequently, and Fandom-Specific Plot for plots common in fan-works. Related to Memetic Mutation. This may also be related to Corrupted Character Copy.

    Advertising 
  • The cereal mascots who deal with trickster children having bloody revenge to get a box, especially the Trix Rabbit.
  • Erin Esurance being a magnet for Rule 34.
  • The kid in the famous seventies "How many licks?" Tootsie Pop commercial being naked and having visible buttcheeks.
  • Tony The Tiger:
    • In particularly brutal parodies, the kids just leech Tony at their bullies, because who is stupid enough to bully a kid who is friends with a talking tiger?
    • Pointing out that Frosted Flakes are extremely impractical in making someone a better athlete, due to their high sugar amount. Especially when people assume that the kids are only good in sports because they ate some.
    • Making jabs at Tony's Furry Fandom.
  • M&M's:
    • Pointing out that the marketing team actually tried to make a green chocolate drop look sexy. And arguably succeeding...
    • Arguing that whenever Red and Yellow are seen destroying an M&M, they are actually mutilating the corpse of a fallen brethren.
  • The Kool-Aid Man bursting into random walls at the sound of Kool-Aid being mentioned (sometimes he doesn't even need that excuse, though).
  • McDonald's: Ronald McDonald is a Monster Clown.
  • Chuck E. Cheese:
    • The reason their pizza tastes so terrible is because the restaurant's namesake is a giant rat who carries diseases.
    • The animatronics are both alive and evil. This was later used for the premise of Five Nights at Freddy's.
    • The restaurant is a nightmare for anyone over 10, and disturbing to play in for anyone under 10. This is so widespread the Suck E. Cheese's trope spun off from it.

    Anime & Manga 
  • Cells at Work!:
    • Focusing on the cells that deal with less glamorous body functions, such as the ones in waste management or childbirth. The CODE BLACK spinoff would eventually deal with the former, though played for drama rather than laughs.
    • Since the cells are humanoid, speculating what their "cells" do to keep them alive.
  • Digimon:
    • The entire franchise is nothing but a cheap knock-off of Pokémon. In actuality, it started off as a Darker and Edgier variant of Tamagotchi, and, since the Mons genre is, well, an entire genre, it has nothing to do with Pokémon aside from being in the same genre. However, since these are the two most well-known examples of the genre, many assume the one that became known in the U.S. shortly after the first was introduced must be a rip-off.
    • Digimon being plain animals or monsters with weapons strapped onto them.
  • Dragon Ball:
    • The battles take numerous episodes.
    • The characters spend a lot of time charging up and screaming during fight scenes.
    • Goku is an Idiot Hero and Saiyans are a Blood Knight race, sure, but parodies like to make him an imbecile in a "Forrest Gump-is-Einstein-in-comparison" caliber who literally only lives to eat and fight and isn't going to win any "father of the year" awards ever.
  • Goblin Slayer is a fantasy counterpart of Doom.note The same actor, Brad Hawkins, did both voice acting for Goblin Slayer and motion capture for Doom Slayer. In fact, Goblin Slayer is very far from being a berserker like Doom Slayer: he wins by using smart tactics and improvised tools, and most of time he doesn't even fights goblins directly and instead sets their lairs on fire, floods them or desinfects them with poisonous gasses.
  • JoJo's Bizarre Adventure:
    • Riffing on the fashion model poses and fabulous clothes the characters are known for.
    • Random 『words』 being put in 『quotation marks 』 to mimic how the series frames key phrases.
    • It's always the work of an enemy Stand.
  • Kimba the White Lion: Originally, people joked about Disney stealing the plot from Kimba to use as a blueprint for The Lion King (1994) without giving any credit to Osamu Tezuka. After Adam Johnston did a video debunking the controversy and talking about what Kimba is really about, most Kimba parodies nowadays involve Kimba being a colonialist white supremacist, an abusive parent, and/or casually carrying his dead father's body around.
  • My Hero Academia:
    • Midoriya cries too much and fights too little.
    • Bakugo being portrayed as a Bastard Boyfriend to Midoriya, with his former bullying turned into (occasionally abusive) Loving Bully tactics.
    • One for All being used as sexual enhancement, "smash" puns notwithstanding.
  • Naruto:
    • Naruto is an annoying, loud Idiot Hero, Sasuke is an exaggerated Emo Teen, and Sakura is a useless fangirl.
    • Hinata is a Stalker with a Crush for Naruto.
    • Hyuuga use their Byakugan for perverse reasons. This eventually made itself into canon in a movie, where an Alternate Universe version of Neji is a pervert who misuses his Byakugan.
    • Poking fun of how Naruto is a Highly Visible Ninja who dresses in orange.
    • Naruto says "Believe It!" all the time. In canon, that was an Abandoned Catchphrase within 30 episodes. Naruto says it a lot in the first Naruto: Ultimate Ninja, but not in the anime itself.
    • Naruto and Sasuke are a couple going through a bad breakup, with Naruto as the Love Martyr.
  • Neon Genesis Evangelion:
    • Shinji being whiny and reluctant to fight to the point where his colleagues need to convince him to "get in the fucking robot". This also comes hand in hand with the supposed irony of him hating being an EVA pilot while he's surrounded by beautiful women and has a job most kids would kill to have a chance at (the actual emotional benefits of any of these things notwithstanding).
    • Asuka as the Only Sane Man between a creepy doll, a crybaby, and a touchy-feely campy menace.
  • One Piece: There is no One Piece. The real treasure was the adventures the Straw Hats had along the way. This is Common Knowledge for two reasons: Oda (and later Whitebeard) clarified that the treasure is indeed real, and Luffy already believes in the latter ideal in-universe, as it makes finding it that much more fun.note That said, the series' little-remembered first movie played this straight with the hoard of a different Legendary Pirate Captain.
  • Pokémon:
    • Exaggerating Ash's Idiot Hero traits.
    • Ash's inability to win a major League tournament. His big win in Sun & Moon put this joke to rest, for some of the fandom, but for others the joke evolved into "A major League that matters".
    • Pokémon begrudgingly using Pokémon Speak around their trainers, but otherwise being fluent Talking Animals.
    • Comic-Book Time seemingly applying only to Ash, or Ash secretly being immortal.
    • Ash being Oblivious to Love.
  • Puella Magi Madoka Magica:
    • Homura is a Stalker with a Crush who steals panties from Madoka and pulls them on head.
    • Jokes concerning Mami's devouring by Charlotte are a staple of Madoka parodies.
  • Sailor Moon:
    • Tuxedo Mask doesn't actually help with anything.
    • Making fun of the girls' skimpy outfits. Part of this is Common Knowledge: the bottom of the Sailor Fuku is a leotard, not panties.
    • Haruka and Michiru being referred to as "cousins", even "Kissing Cousins", during clearly romantic moments as a jab at the censorship in the Cloverway dub.
    • The Sailors' transformations take as long as the viewer watches the stock footage instead of being instantaneous as in-universe. This one is shared with the magical girl genre in general.
  • Speed Racer: Everyone talks really, really fast.
  • Strike Witches: Jokes about how the girls don't wear any pants. Others take it further and say the Witches aren't wearing any underwear either.
  • Yu-Gi-Oh!:
    • People treat a children's card game like it's Serious Business. This is usually coupled with the one character who treats said game like it is, but whom usually is either ignored or mocked by the others for not playing along.
    • Calling Duel Monsters "A Children's Card Game" instead of its proper name, a practice carried over from fans of Yu-Gi-Oh! The Abridged Series.

    Comic Books 
  • In general: Superheroes cause more problems than they solve, and the villains would stop attacking if the heroes stopped egging them on.
    • Similarly, the real villain of a Superhero universe is actually the comic book industry itself (or a metaphor for it); artificially reviving and modifying characters for entertainment value, using them as jingoistic propaganda, advertising becoming a parody of celebrity culture, legions of fanboys arguing over which version of their lives was better (and if they're Running the Asylum, actively getting in a reality-warping edit war over it), you name it. The original comic version of The Boys used this among other things as its premise.
  • Aquaman: Aquaman is useless due to his powers being sea-based. He isn't useful in most scenarios and his ability to talk to fish is unimpressive. This joke comes from the Superfriends cartoon and has been referenced (as well as deconstructed) in official DC works.
  • Batman:
    • Due to Batman's famous penchant for having young male sidekicks, you can expect to see jokes about gay men or pedophiles. These jokes have made their way into official media such as Batman and Harley Quinn.
    • Batman is a billionaire who beats up hobos— and he'd be better off using his billions to invest in social programs (He already does this, but Gotham is Gotham so it doesn't always work).
    • More commonly found in the fandom, Batman has a breakdown whenever someone mentions his (dead) parents.
  • The Flash: Jokes about the Flash being a mediocre lover due to his speed are commonplace. It's to the point where various DC media have referenced the joke. Jokes about the Flash being a literal human vibrator are somewhat less common, but have been said.
  • Similarly to The Flash above, Lucky Luke is joked as being a mediocre lover due to his ability to "shoot faster than his shadow", giving birth to countless jokes about Lucky Luke being a premature ejaculator.note It actually comes (he he he) from fans seeing a Double Entendre in said catchphrase: "l'homme qui tire plus vite que son ombre" does literally mean "the man who shoots faster than his shadow", but can also technically be translated as "the man who fucks faster than his shadow"
  • The Smurfs:
    • The Smurfs are a Communist society due to them having assigned jobs that are even in their names, Papa Smurf wearing red, and the acronym "Soviet Men Under Red Father".
    • Being the Trope Namer for The Smurfette Principle, a lot of jokes are made about the implications of a whole society with only one woman.
  • Superman:
    • Clark Kenting never fooled anybody, and Clark's really bad at hiding his powers. Either that, or his Superman personality keeps bleeding into his work at the Daily Planet.
    • Clark is every bit the Smug Super that Lex Luthor thought he was.
    • Lois Lane deliberately getting into danger just so Superman will have to come and save her.
  • Wonder Woman:
    • Lesbian and bisexual jokes because Wonder Woman comes from an all-female island. This is an Unbuilt Trope as Wonder Woman has been Ambiguously Bi since the 1940s (before becoming explicitly so in the 2010s) and Themyscira has had several confirmed lesbian couples (including Wonder Woman's mother).
    • Wonder Woman allows herself to get tied up a lot because she enjoys it.
  • Marvel Comics: Making fun of the fact that nearly all of Marvels major characters live and operate in New York City.
  • Captain America: Cap is comedically behind the times due to having spent most of the modern era frozen in ice. This idea was used to some extent in The Ultimates, but Cap has otherwise never been anything but accepting of others.
  • For comic books in general, jokes about the Death Is Cheap trope (Jean Grey of the X-Men is a particularly common target of these jokes, due to just how often she has cheated death).

    Comic Strips 
  • Calvin and Hobbes:
    • Hobbes is just a toy and Calvin is a mentally ill boy who simply hallucinates otherwise.
    • Calvin is a violent murderous Enfant Terrible.
    • Calvin and Hobbes crashed and died after riding their sled off in the final strip.
  • Garfield:
    • Jon murdered Lyman.
    • Garfield is an Eldritch Abomination afflicted with Horror Hunger.
  • Peanuts:
    • Charlie Brown's baldness is due to him being a chemotherapy patient.
    • The typical football prank ending with either Chuck or someone else making a fool of Lucy instead of the other way around. The 1981 special It's Magic, Charlie Brown had this happen throughout the third act, due to Chuck having temporarily been turned invisible.
    • Peppermint Patty and Marcie being a lesbian couple, due to them being tomboys and Marcie's penchant for calling Patty "Sir" — even though canonically they're both interested in Charlie Brown.

Alternatively, that large pumpkin Lucy carved up at the beginning of It's the Great Pumpkin, Charlie Brown is the Great Pumpkin, hence why he didn't come that night.     Fairy Tales 
  • The Boy Who Cried Wolf:
    • The boy never cried wolf in the first place; the villagers just kept coming at the worst time and assumed he was pranking them.
    • The villagers kill the boy and then lie that a wolf got him.
    • The villagers firing the boy and replacing him with a more reliable person to watch the sheep.
  • Cinderella:
    • Given his method of tracking down Cinderella, the prince has a foot fetish and/or is hopelessly faceblind.
    • Cinderella complaining about how uncomfortable glass slippers are.
  • The Frog Prince: Kissing the prince turns the girl into a frog,note which Disney utilized for the plot of The Princess and the Frog makes the frog prince lose his remaining sapience, or revealing that the frog body was his true form all along.
  • Little Red Riding Hood:
    • The wolf is a metaphor for a sultry lover, or "Little" Red actively pursues him.note Some early versions of the story do have sexual subtext, such as the wolf inviting Little Red to bed before she escapes, which makes the "stranger danger" aesop even clearer.
    • Little Red is an Enfant Terrible and/or a Fille Fatale.
    • The woodsman wanted an excuse to cut something open.
    • Little Red's family just really hates wolves.
    • Little Red brings a gun and just shoots the wolf.
    • The wolf being a Wholesome Crossdresser who enjoys dressing in Granny's nightgown, popularized by Shrek.
  • Sleeping Beauty:
    • Sleeping Beauty is narcoleptic. This joke made it into Shrek and Ralph Breaks the Internet (itself a bit of Self-Deprecation by Disney).
    • The prince takes advantage of Sleeping Beauty while she's comatose. This is an Unbuilt Trope as the earliest versions of the story, most famously Sun, Moon, and Talia, include the protagonist being impregnated in her sleep (with the kiss being a product of censorship).
  • Snow White:
    • The prince is a necrophiliac.
    • Snow White sleeps with the dwarfs during her time with them.
  • The Big Bad Wolf is an innocent guy, really. (Either he's lying through his teeth or the other characters in the story really did set him up.)
  • Goldilocks and the Three Bears:
    • Goldilocks being a Spoiled Brat who either ends up getting mauled or at least kicked out by the bears.
    • Mama Bear and Papa Bear Sleeping Single is because they are about to divorce.
  • Jack and the Beanstalk:
    • The giants instantly getting revenge on Jack for taking their goose. Also, Jack being a selfish and shortsighted brat who just wanted the goose.
    • The story being presented as a home invasion plot from the perspective of the giant.
    • The "magic beans" were fake all along, and the guy who sold them to Jack was a con artist.
  • Rapunzel
    • Rapunzel's parents being awful people for trading their unborn child for some lettuce. note This is due to Values Dissonance, apparently pregnancy cravings used to be Serious Business
    • Where does Rapunzel go to poop?
    • Rapunzel hitting her head at the base of the window after the Prince climbs it and she's weighed down note Though apparently, being able to climb someone's hair is Truth in Television, could hair grow that long
    • Rapunzel's hair growing from some other less pleasant part of her body, such as her nose, armpits, or pubic area.
  • The Princess and the Pea:
    • The Princess suffering from butterfly disease, her skin is so thin that even the slightest touch harms her.
    • The Princess isn't actually a princess, she simply couldn't sleep due to stress.
    • Pointing out how mad the Prince and his mother must be to come up with such a ridiculous test.

    Films — Live Action 
  • The Adventures of Milo and Otis: All the alleged animal abuse that apparently went on behind the scenes.
  • Avatar:
    • The film just being Pocahontas in space.
    • The Na'vi exhibiting stereotypical cat behavior such as purring or playing with yarn.
  • Battleship: Making action films based on other board games, such as Connect Four: The Movie.
  • Bill & Ted: The phonebooth-meets-Time Machine being a rip-off of Doctor Who.
  • E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial
    • Jokes about guns being replaced with walkie-talkies in the re-release, often seen as a case of Political Correctness Gone Mad.
    • The Accidental Nightmare Fuel of ET coming off more horrifying than cute.
  • Godzilla:
    • Godzilla (and Kaiju in general) have an obsession with rampaging through Tokyo. Even when there's movies that have him not targeting Tokyo. Similarly, there's the joke that he and other Kaiju are literally People in Rubber Suits.
    • Mocking the hilarious uselessness of the Japanese Self-Defense Forces when it comes to fending off Kaiju.
    • Jokes about how the construction companies are making a killing with the amount of work they need to do after the average Kaiju rampage.
    • Questioning what ends up happening with the waste matter of such large creatures.
    • Jokes about how the Japanese citizenry eventually becomes completely desensitized to having Godzilla wreaking havoc once a day and twice on Tuesdays.
  • Gremlins: Questioning the third rule about not feeding Mogwai after midnight, as the film never says when it's okay to feed them again, thus meaning it's always after midnight.
  • Harry Potter: Parodies about Hermione hitting puberty and becoming a Dude Magnet are a dime-a-dozen due to her actress' growth as the films progressed. Often goes hand-in-hand with having the male cast's puberty be a lot more awkward.
  • Home Alone: Kevin McCallister being a budding sociopath with an interest in booby traps not dissimilar to John "Jigsaw" Kramer (as a matter of fact, some jokes are about how Kevin is Jigsaw and this is his Start of Darkness). It's gotten to the point that some of the film's actors (including Macaulay Culkin) have made parody videos with the plot of "Evil Kevin, decades later" (or talked about it in some circles, like "The Tonight Show") and Better Watch Out is essentially an extended Played for Horror homage.
  • James Bond:
    • The villain in any Bond parody will almost always be a parody of Donald Pleasence's version of Blofeld in You Only Live Twice.
    • Bond's gadgets being used for sexual purposes. (Redundant Parody, since this actually happened several times in the films' more comedic moments.)
    • The villain putting Bond into a ridiculously elaborate death trap and letting him escape from it instead of killing him in a rapid and mundane way.
    • The sexual punning common in Bond Girls' names being taking to ridiculous lengths, or simply being replaced by blunt obscenity.
  • Jurassic Park:
    • Hammond deliberately intended the park to fail and for everyone to be eaten.
    • The Tyrannosaurus rex and/or raptors being portrayed with feathers to be more accurate, usually looking as silly as possible.
  • Labyrinth: David Bowie's huge crotch bulge.
  • The Shape of Water: Jokes about the Interspecies Romance being a blatant fetish for the director.
  • Star Wars:
    • Darth Vader acting like a stereotypical dad towards Luke.
      • Jeffrey Brown's cartoon collections starting with Darth Vader and Son, which are officially-published, but obviously non-canon, use this concept as the jumping off point.
    • The Storm Troopers have lousy aim, sometimes incredibly lousy.
    • Ever since the George Lucas Altered Version of A New Hope changed the classic "Han shoots first" version to Greedo shooting first but missing even if he was at point-blank range, jokes about his crappy marksmanship have been made, even in official SW media like the LEGO shorts.
    • Jar Jar Binks is either violently killed, or turns out to be a Sith Lord (the idea that he was originally written as the latter is an extremely popular fan theory).
    • Speaking of George Lucas Altered Versions, spoofing the numerous versions of the saga by having George himself or an expy of him change things around because he can.
    • The Death Star was poorly-designed. This one made it into Rogue One as the rebellion's last great middle finger to the Empire.
    • Endless "gay jokes" about C-3PO. Bonus points if he and R2-D2 are a gay couple.
  • Titanic (1997):
    • Rose and Jack could have both fit on the large wooden slab so the latter didn't have to die. While the slab was indeed large enough for the both of them, it does briefly show that Jack does try and get on with Rose resulting the slab almost flipping over and sinking, so it wasn't able to support the weight of the two of them.
    • Suggesting romance films based on other disasters, such as 9/11.
  • Willy Wonka & the Chocolate Factory:
    • Mr. Wonka is a child murderer who deliberately staged the naughty children's "accidents" on purpose, and the Oompa-Loompas are slaves.note The latter is not too far from the truth, given that the Oompa Loompas were African pygmies in the first version of the original book, before Bowdlerization. The 2013 West End musical and its Broadway Retool actually run with the former concept by having some of the naughty kids threatened with, or actually suffering, Death by Adaptation for Black Comedy purposes.
    • The factory being sued for child endangerment.
    • Grandpa Joe is a lazy jerk for acting immobile for decades, only to suddenly be able to get up and dance when Charlie finds a golden ticket.
  • The Wizard of Oz:
    • Glinda is a Bitch in Sheep's Clothing who toyed with Dorothy for kicks by making her run around Oz, without telling her how the Ruby Slippers work. In Dystopian Oz parodies, she might also be a tyrant who wanted Dorothy to destroy her political enemies.
    • The Tin Man as Camp Gay.
    • The Wicked Witch being an idiot for keeping random buckets of water in her lair, knowing full well water is her Weaksauce Weakness. (The crossover Tom and Jerry and the Wizard of Oz made an explanation for this a major plot point: The cat and mouse have to figure out how to steal some water from the sole well on the property to help Dorothy and her friends.)

    Literature

  • Alice's Adventures in Wonderland:
    • Alice is either mad or a drug user.
    • Author Lewis Carroll was a pedophile who wrote the books to groom the "real" Alice.
  • Animorphs: Making fun of the Body Horror of the transformations on the covers. Ironically, most people who make those jokes haven't read the books and don't even know that the textual descriptions of the transformations are far more graphic and intentionally disturbing.
  • Diary of a Wimpy Kid: Greg is a sociopath, and Manny is an Enfant Terrible.
  • The Divine Comedy: The poem is all just one self indulgent fanfiction written by Dante.
  • Dr. Seuss:
    • The Cat in the Hat is a creep for breaking into a house with two children home alone.
    • The titular Green Eggs and Ham are rotten, hence their color. Alternatively, Sam-I-Am is either a drug dealer or wants to have gay sex with Guy-Am-I.
    • The Sneetches were already racist to other species even before McBean's machine did anything, and the only thing they learned from the ordeal is to stop being so prejudiced towards each other. And probably not to trust McBean's species on top of everyone else.
    • The Grinch developed cardiomegaly after his heart grew three sizes.
    • The Lorax quitting his Nature Spirit job and becoming a greedy eco-unfriendly capitalist.
  • Fifty Shades of Grey:
    • Jokes about Christian being the 'wrong' kind of fetishist (feeding, inflation, baby play, etc.) and Ana falling in love with him anyway.
    • Christian being the other kind of Dungeon Master.
  • Goosebumps:
    • The series trying to make mundane things such as sponges scary.
    • The constant use of Cat Scare cliffhangers at the end of every chapter, where it looks like something scary is happening, but it turns out to be something mundane, is commonly mocked.
  • The Giving Tree: The boy and the tree are in an abusive relationship, and the tree has Stockholm Syndrome.
  • Harry Potter:
    • Dumbledore, instead of being an Eccentric Mentor, is a delusional madman who endangers the lives of children on a daily basis.
    • The Hufflepuffs aren't important.
    • Ron is either useless or evil.
  • Land of Oz:
    • Oz is a benevolent dictatorship.
    • Ozma, Glinda, and especially the Wizard are all morally dubious.
    • Ozma and Dorothy are a couple.
    • Oz is a Crapsaccharine World.
  • Lord of the Rings:
    • The Eagles could have taken Frodo to Mordor.
    • The elves either work for Santa, or get a job portraying Vulcans in Star Trek.
  • Sherlock Holmes:
    • Sherlock and Watson are gay.
    • Sherlock's use of a Sherlock Scan leads to an outlandish, wrong conclusion.
    • Values Dissonance and Technology Marches On leads Sherlock to send innocent men to jail.
  • Peter Pan:
    • Peter being some sort of child murderer, either tricking the Darlings into jumping to their death, culling any Lost Boys who grow too old, or being an Angel of Death who leads dying children to the afterlife (Neverland). Notably, the first draft of the book was going to go in this direction, and the book's version of Peter has some less heroic behavior that the adaptations tend to leave out, making this a bit of a Redundant Parody.
    • Tinkerbell getting killed in some sort of insect-like fashion, such as being swatted, sprayed with bug spray, or eaten by a frog.
    • Peter aging physically, but mentally staying a Manchild.
    • Wendy, John, and Michael staying in Neverland, reasoning that it would be better than going back to mundane reality. This was already a plot point in the book, caused by the siblings gradually losing their memories of home the longer they stayed, and is incorporated into the plot of the unofficial sequel Hook.
  • Tolkien's Legendarium: "Pipeweed" is marijuana and Gandalf and the hobbits are stoners.
  • Twilight:
    • The Cullens sparkle in the sunlight instead of burning like they 'should'.
    • The rivalry between Team Edward and Team Jacob.
    • Edward and Bella's single-minded obsession with each other.
    • Famous vampire hunters massacring the Cullens in the middle of the plot.
    • Jacob not owning a shirt.
    • Jacob being a pedophile for imprinting on the child of his crush.
  • Winnie-the-Pooh:
    • Pooh and friends' character quirks represent either a spectrum of mental illnesses, or the effects of different drugs.
    • Pooh being addicted to and/or getting high off of honey as if it were a drug.

    Live-Action TV 
  • The Brady Bunch: The Brady kids are incestuous (at least towards the ones from the opposite side of the family). This would be incorporated into A Very Brady Sequel with some heavy Ship Tease between Marcia and Greg.
  • Dateline: An actual Predator from the 1987 film showing up to be intercepted by Chris Hansen.
  • Doctor Who:
    • Replacing the TARDIS with a porta-potty, sometimes even calling it the TURDIS. Alternatively, having someone mistake it for a porta-potty. (This even happened in the first issue of the Twelfth Doctor's monthly comic.)
    • The Doctor's Sonic Screwdriver having various sexual applications (as was alluded to at the end of Doctor Who: The Curse of Fatal Death).
    • Daleks can't climb stairs (also a Fandom-Enraging Misconception, as it was strongly implied to be untrue in 1965, and explicitly shown to be untrue in 1988, 2005, and several more recent occasions).
    • All the classic-era UNIT characters are Ambiguously Gay.
    • The Doctor is shagging all his companions.
    • Female companions are always Ms. Fanservice Damsels In Distress who are incapable of doing anything except screaming and asking the Doctor what's going on. Also a Fandom-Enraging Misconception.
    • [UNPOPULAR PUBLIC FIGURE] is a Dalek.
    • The existence of a Bachelor (or more rarely, an Associate) to go with the Doctor and the Master, usually portrayed as more inept than the latter two. Occasionally, the Bachelor is said to be the reality show contestant of the same name, with each season's Bachelor regarded as his incarnations.
  • Friends:
    • All the characters sleep with each other.
    • Pointing out how the diversity of the show does not accurately reflect the diversity of New York City.
  • Game of Thrones:
    • Putting so much focus on the nudity and graphic sex scenes that the story is treated as pretty much just incidental. There's almost always references to incest as well (and not just with the characters who are canonically incestuous).
    • Almost every notable character dies at some point.
  • Happy Days:
    • Fonzie actually has superpowers rather than being incredibly competent and lucky the way he is in canon.
    • Joke explanations for Chuck disappearing off the show. Common ones include Fonzie or Mork having killed him.
  • How I Met Your Mother: Ted's kids getting annoyed at his constant tangents that seemingly have nothing to do with their mother. This was even a joke in a non-canon promo shown at Comic-Con before the final season aired.
  • I Love Lucy: Ricky Ricardo being an abusive husband, usually because he just got that fed up of Lucy's antics.
  • Laverne & Shirley:
    • The titular characters are lesbians and/or promiscuous.
    • People also joke about their neighbours Lenny and Squiggy being gay.
    • Laverne's "L" she wears on her shirt actually stands for "lesbian".
  • Star Trek:
    • Kirk's Large Ham tendencies will be exaggerated and he will either be dating Spock or even more of a ladies' man than canonically. Parodies of Kirk also tend to make him less competent and intelligent than the canon version.
    • Spock or Data saying something uncharacteristically snarky and/or perverted but with their usual Sesquipedalian Loquaciousness.
    • Writing Scotty or Chekov's accent phonetically ("I don't have the par" instead of "power" is a common one for Scotty").
    • Wesley Crusher and/or Deanna Troi is whiny and useless.
    • Joking about how the Enterprise doesn't have bathrooms and suggesting that they transport the waste out of your body.
    • Making any catchphrase or tagline (e.g. "Make it so", "To boldly go..") sexual.
    • Jokes about how in Star Trek: Voyager, everyone would've gotten home faster if they didn't explore.
    • For that matter, Captain Janeway being some kind of comic-book supervillain.
    • Red Shirts dying by the starship-load, and jokes about anyone wearing red (except Scotty, who is given Memetic Badass status instead) being a dead man walking.
    • Exaggerating Janeway's love of coffee, Picard's love of Earl Gray, or Troi's love of chocolate and/or ice cream.
    • Having Data be either a complete lust-ball or, on the flip side, absolutely clueless about sex.
  • Super Sentai/Power Rangers:
    • The team's "Super Sentai" Stance being outlandish or impractical, often with the explosion in the back of their pose actually harming them.
    • The team bickers so much that they can't properly use All Your Powers Combined or pilot their Humongous Mecha.
    • Mighty Morphin' Power Rangers-exclusive: The Unfortunate Implications of the Yellow Ranger being Asian and the Black Ranger being of African descent. Often further parodied with other races, such as a Native American Red Ranger or an Anglo-Saxon White Ranger.
  • Ultraman: The military was completely useless to Ultraman handling the Monster of the Week. Addressed in several series, where one member tries the Holding Out for a Hero routine and fails due to Ultraman's need to fight alongside humanity instead of fighting their battles for them.

    Multiplatform Franchises 
  • Fate Series:
    • Saber is the only character they have. This was used to inspire "Mysterious Heroine X" in the Nasuverse spoof Back Alley Satsuki, and her return in Fate/Grand Order made it so she was actively trying to kill any Saberface still around.
    • Astolfo is gay and promiscuous because he's a cheery Wholesome Crossdresser.
    • Expectations of a historically male public figure inevitably turning out to be a girl, given all of the Historical Gender Flips across installments.

     Music 
  • There's no irony in Alanis Morissette's "Ironic" song.
  • Boy Bands are made up of brainless clones that have no distinguishing traits aside from hair color or Informed Ability. Similarly, Girl Groups and especially Idol Singers in general care nothing for their fans and only get by on looks.
  • Justin Bieber's high voice is the result of him either being late for puberty, castrated, or a girl in disguise.
  • Michael Jackson: Claiming he turned into a white guy as he got older, due to the lightening of his skin.note In reality, Jackson had vitiligo and still identified as a black man as his pigmentation changed. Also is often portrayed as a child molester, especially in works made at the time of the accusations.
  • Jason Derulo has to say his name at the beginning of every song he sings.

    Mythology and Folklore 
  • The Judeo-Christian God being portrayed as a "Sky Fairy" or a "Sky Daddy".
  • Classical Mythology: Zeus is a pervert who can't keep it in his pants.
  • King Arthur breaks the sword in the stone and/or has been using a fake the whole time.
  • Robin Hood stealing from the rich, only to steal from the people he just gave the money to because they technically count as "rich" now. Alternatively, stealing from people who excitedly yell "we're rich!" regardless of their actual wealth, or lying about the whole ordeal and keeping the riches for himself.
  • Santa Claus:
    • Santa being a creep for spying on sleeping children and breaking into people's houses at night.
    • His Christmas Elves being slaves. This joke is especially prominent when bringing up the relationship between his Sinterklaas counterpart and his helper Zwarte Piet.
    • The reindeer being cooked and made into venison.
    • Santa was real, but he died messily long ago, because nobody could survive the flight speed he had to reach in order to cruise through the whole world in a single night.
    • Climate change in the North Pole forced Santa to relocate his workshop. Depending on if this is used as a "why Santa isn't real anymore" answer, this has the optional ending of them not being able to evacuate in time.
    • Santa hates the poor, which is why kids from low-income houses don't recieve presents as often.
    • Santa has long given up on gifting presents to good children, and will either give presents to everyone regardless of how good they were or send everyone coal.

  • The Muppet Show:
    • Kermit and Miss Piggy being in an abusive relationship.
    • Kermit and Miss Piggy having a half-pig half-frog child, usually hideously deformed.
  • Sesame Street:
    • Bert and Ernie being a gay couple, which Sesame Workshop had to clear up several times.
    • With today's increased awareness of childhood obesity, it's not uncommon to accuse Cookie Monster of being executive-meddled into a "Veggie Monster", something the show actually referenced.
    • Cookie Monster is literally addicted to cookies.
    • Having the characters deal with "real" urban street problems, like vulgar neighbors, gang violence, or trying to make a living in a dead-end job. Avenue Q (starring Muppet alumni) took this premise and ran with it, even including the "gay Bert and Ernie" (though one-sided) and "addict Cookie Monster" jokes in its production.
    • Elmo is an Ax-Crazy Enfant Terrible.

  • Romeo and Juliet were two immature and irrational teenagers, and their love was shallow. (Arguably, that was the point—their love was genuine, but they are kind of dumb, innocent kids who tragically got thrown into the feuding families' mess).

    • Ken is Camp Gay.

    • Barbie's many jobs are either because she's using them as a cover up for her real profession, or because there's in infinite amount of Barbies doing a variety of jobs out there.
  • Furby:
    • Furbies are actually evil.
    • Comparisons to Mogwais because of their ears.
  • LEGO: LEGO bricks being painful to step on. This joke made it into LEGO Dimensions and The LEGO Movie 2: The Second Part, and it was referenced in the song "Dance of Doom" in The LEGO Ninjago Movie.
  • My Little Pony:
    • Ponyland/Dream Valley being all-female. This is Common Knowledge as all generations besides G3 (2003-2009) featured stallions and colts.
    • Ponyland/Dream Valley as a Sugar Bowl. Alternatively, Ponyland/Dream Valley as a Crapsaccharine World, due to how 1980s/G1 works presented it.
