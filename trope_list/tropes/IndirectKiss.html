Indirect Kiss

Go To

Edit Locked

  • Main
  • Laconic
  • Create New

"I know you didn't wanna kiss me, but when I was taking a hit off that bottle before, I backwashed in it. I mean, like, really big-time."
— McKenas Cole, Alias
Advertisement:

Issues of physical contact taken to a logical extreme. The surprise is when someone is offered food or drink by someone they like, then realizes that swapping spit residue is similar to a kiss, albeit indirectly.

During The Hays Code era of American films, a couple smoking cigarettes together, particularly if they share one cigarette or light one with another, would suggest they were up to something more than kissing.

A form of this notion, the sharing of a drinking glass with a lover as an intimate act, appears in a treatise on romance by the Roman poet Ovid, making this Older Than Feudalism.

See also Sweetheart Sipping, Date Crêpe.

    Literature 
  • A rather strange example of indirect sex occurs in John Donne's poem "The Flea". A man tries to get his girlfriend in the mood but she's having none of it. He pulls a tick/louse/some bloodsucking insect from his pubic hair and puts to her skin so that it feeds. He then argues that since their bodily fluids have now mixed in the tick it's like they've already had sex, so they might as well do it for real anyway, because it makes no difference. Needless to say, she squashes the tick.

    Of course, this is also a prime example of Get Thee to a Nunnery, as the poem repeatedly mentions "sucking". Back in the day, the long "s" would have resembled an "f".
  • In Shards of Honor by Lois McMaster Bujold:
    • The Hero and Heroine give CPR to another character, and at one point they have to change because her arms are tired. She has a silent ironic laugh at the "parody of a kiss"
    • Done again and heartbreakingly in the same novel when the heroine decides to send the hero a physical letter rather than an electronic one because "it was as close to a touch as they could come."
  • This trope is explored at some length in Longus' Daphnis and Chloe, owing to the woeful sexual inexperience of the two titular characters. Being written in the 2nd century AD makes this, well, rather old.
  • In Kelley Armstrong's Exit Strategy, Jack smokes constantly while Nadia has quit smoking. Jack lets Nadia take puffs off his cigarette, since she won't smoke a full cig but she can rationalize sharing as not really smoking. Neither of them ever discuss it, but the third point in the love triangle notices and gets nasty towards Jack about it. After it's pointed out to her, Nadia is uncomfortable with the intimacy of sharing a cig.
  • Not a straight example of this trope, but possibly an early proto-form of it: in Rainbow Valley, Rosemary West can't help but notice that Rev. Meredith put his lips on the same spot of a tree-bark-cup that she did when she drank from it earlier, and thinks about how she was told that when that happened, the destinies of the two involved will be linked together...
  • In The Name of the Wind Kvothe and his love interest share an apple, which he explains is, "as close to kissing, if you've never done it before," or something to that effect.
  • In Midnight's Children, the narrator describes seeing a Bollywood movie that got around the conservative social conventions of the day by having the hero kiss an apple, then pass it to the heroine so that she can kiss the same spot. For the pre-teen narrator it ends up being more erotic than if they had simply kissed in the first place.
  • A version of the legend of the city of Ys mentions that Dahut-Ahès, the scandalous princess, makes her new-lover-of-the-evening drink from the cup she drunk from and bite from the fruit she bit from.
  • Invoked and averted in Noble House, where Quillan Gornt stubs out a cigarette given to him by his ex-mistress, because he does not want "the taint of her lips". When he said the relationship was over, he really meant it.
  • In one of the Left Behind books, a character wipes away a rogue cookie crumb from his girlfriend's mouth and then absent-mindedly licks it off his finger. The significance of this is discussed in a Slacktivist review;
    "For our protagonists and readers who come from this no-touching-during-courtship subculture, Buck's actions during the Cookie Incident were bold, shocking, even risqué. Thumb to lip contact  that's third base! His thumb touched her mouth and then his own  that's almost like kissing, the sort of thing proper courtship says ought to be saved for the wedding night."
  • Lampshaded in The Bronze Horseman by Paullina Simons. Alexander offers a cigarette to Tatiana, but she refuses as she doesn't smoke. A friend of Tatiana who suspects their Secret Relationship asks, "Are you sure you don't want it? It's been in his mouth."
  • Some illustrations from Paradise Lost, particularly one by William Blake, show Satan as a serpent feeding Eve the Forbidden Fruit with his mouth.

Considering the direction that the series took regarding their relationship as it progressed, this is remarkably subtle.

    • Elaine became frustrated at the mixed signals a man was sending her at the gym, such as wiping off the neck of her water bottle before drinking from it.
    • Another episode has George being berated for double-dipping a chip. "It's like putting your whole mouth in the bowl!"
  • Buffy the Vampire Slayer. Anya and Xander are sharing a tub of ice cream with a single spoon, griping about how they can hear Willow and Kennedy making love. When we cut back to them, they're making out on the floor.
  • Averted in Person of Interest. As part of their ongoing Homoerotic Subtext Root pinches Shaw's soda, but Shaw just wipes it off in irritation before drinking from it again.
  • Supernatural. In "Girl Next Door", a pre-teen Sam Winchester saves the Girl Next Door from school bullies. She treats a head injury he received in the process by pressing a soda can on it, then she drinks from the can. From the grin on Sam's face at this point, he's aware of the subtext.
    • One episode had an indirect magic kiss. Sirens are Shapeshifting Seducers who control their victims with a kiss. They can do this because of a chemical in their saliva. So when Dean lets his new friend Nick drink out of his flask and doesn't wipe it afterwards...
  • In Gran Hotel, Julio and Alicia cannot kiss in public because they are having a secret Interclass Romance. So, they share cigarettes and he remarks on how it is almost as if they are openly kissing.

    Music 
  • HoneyWorks' "Tokyo Summer Session" has Souta offer his crush, Akari, a sip of his drink to invoke this trope. Akari isn't fooled, but she accepts the drink anyway.
    Akari: I'm thirsty.
    Souta: Want a sip of this?
    Akari: Is this what they call an indirect kiss?
    Souta:  you noticed?

    Theatre 
  • The stage directions of Bugsy Malone more than once call for Bugsy to kiss his own fingertips and then press them to his Love Interest's nose.

    Video Games 
  • Cloud's immaturity in Final Fantasy VII is showcased in his reluctance to perform the Kiss of Life on a little girl.
  • In a cutscene in Disgaea 2, the party is surrounded by monsters and attempt to use a magic flute to distract them. Adell tries playing it first, but is proven to have no musical talent whatsoever, so Rozalin takes the flute from him and puts it to her lips — at which point she realizes the significance of this act. She then forcefully discards the flute into her servant's forehead. Axel later finds the flute and plays it, but this trope isn't mentioned.
  • In Season 2 of Batman: The Telltale Series, Harley Quinn playfully offers Bruce a slurp from a slushy she just drank, which makes "John Doe" (this universe's version of Joker) jealous. If Bruce accepts, John angrily takes the cup and finishes it himself. Not accepting leads him to happily swiping it from Harley in order to get it himself.

    Visual Novels 
  • In the CLANNAD visual novel, the hopelessly shy Ryou takes a grain of rice off Tomoya's lips and eats it. When she realizes the implications, she gets so flustered that she runs away. If the player tries to go for her more confident twin sister Kyou, Ryou does the same thing a few days later, but doesn't mind at all, a hint that she is trying to act like Kyou.
  • Referenced by name during two separate instances in (P)lanets. In the first instance, the main character shares her half-eaten sandwich with a Love Interest; the second instance happens when a different love interest steals one of her half bitten rice balls.
  • Katawa Shoujo: in Rin's route, she and Hisao share a cigarette and Hisao thinks about how they're sharing indirect kisses.
  • Go! Go! Nippon! is a visual novel aimed specifically at Occidental Otaku who want to visit Japan, and as such has plenty of anime and manga tropes, including this one. The indirect kiss happens by sharing a drink with one of the two sisters who are hosting the protagonist.
  • In Tokimeki Memorial, if the protagonist meets ace swimmer Nozomi Kiyokawa for the first time as she does her daily 50 kilometers run, she'll offer him some of her juice. As she'll leave him, the protagonist will realize and gleefully mention to himself he had an indirect kiss this way.

    Webcomics 
  • Reika of Red String wonders if sharing a juice box can be considered an indirect kiss.

    Western Animation 
  • In Adventure Time, Finn and Flame Princess use this by passing a rock back and forth, kissing it. Of course, they have to, considering Flame Princess' unstable state could cause the world to be destroyed if they get too romantic.
    • Well, that and the first real kiss they had burnt Finn's mouth.
  • Beavis And Butthead once got kicked out of a lingerie shop because they went around touching all the crotches of the panties and insides of the bras, figuring all of them will eventually be bought and worn by hot chicks.
  • Happens to Vlad Masters from Danny Phantom in "Maternal Instincts". "The Backwash Incident" is one of the reasons why he wants to kill Jack.
  • Averted in the Futurama episode "Roswell That Ends Well", in which a stereotypical 1950's diner has a sign on the door telling patrons, "One teen to a malt please."
  • In the Generator Rex and Ben 10 crossover special, Holiday gives one to a comatose Agent Six by kissing the glass covering of his bed, close to his cheek.
  • Steven Universe: "An Indirect Kiss" has the eponymous indirect kiss happen between Connie and Steven, when Steven trades the rest of his juice box to Connie in exchange for letting him try on her glasses. He even admits it's mostly backwash at that point. This would seem like a footnote to the episode, which is mostly about Steven trying to gain Swiss Army Tears, except it turns out that he actually has healing spit, which cures Connie's astigmatism.
  • In one episode of X-Men after Gambit saves Rogue from a sentinel he asks for a kiss, because of her powers touching them zaps their energy, so she puts her gloved hand over his mouth and kisses her hand.
