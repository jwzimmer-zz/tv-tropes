Girls with Moustaches

Go To

Edit Locked

("It's the beards.")
Advertisement:

This trope applies to any female character who sports a moustache or beard at some point. Since it is extremely unusual for human females to have large amounts of facial hair, this is almost inevitably Played for Laughs, with the Unfortunate Implications that can go along with it.

The most common variant involves a woman wearing a fake moustache, possibly as part of a Sweet Polly Oliver, or temporarily growing one due to some form of Applied Phlebotinum. Sometimes it may even just be a part of an Imagine Spot. Permanently moustachioed women are rarer. If they do appear, expect them to be a part of The Freakshow as "The Bearded Lady". This is also a stereotype of Nerd girls.

If female dwarves appear, expect jokes to be made about them having beards, even if they normally don't.

Not to be confused with Moustache de Plume.

  • In Ranma ½, when Akane eats the super soba. This soup is full of male hormones, making facial hair growth a side effect. At least the whiskers drop off once the magic wears off. Although in her case they are literal whiskers; six or eight long strands of hair jutting from her cheeks, like a dog or a cat.

    Arts 
  • Frida Kahlo had a visible moustache in most of her self-portraits. When comparing her self-portraits to photographs of the artist, one can see that while she did have some peach-fuzz on her upper lip and a unibrow, she deliberately accentuated these traits in her paintings.
  • Magdalena Ventura, an Italian woman who suffered of hirsutism and who had her portrait painted by Jusepe de Ribera.

    Myths & Religion 
  • According to The Other Wiki, Guanyin, the Buddhist bodhisattva of compassion, is sometimes depicted as having a mustache despite being usually considered female. Some images displayed Guanyin attributes of both genders, in accordance with the Lotus Sutra.
  • Wilgefortis, also know as Kümmernis, was an apocryphal Christian saint, venerated in The Late Middle Ages. The story goes that she was a Christian princess, forced to marry a heathen by her father. To avert this fate, she prayed for her face to look repulsive. Her wish was granted by her miraculously growing a beard, whereupon her father had her crucified. The myth probably arose from depictions of the crucified Christ wearing a long robe, which to some audiences would have looked like a woman's dress.
  • The Yoruba goddess Oya is said to sprout a beard before going to battle.

    Tabletop Games 
  • Dungeons & Dragons:
    • The spell "Silverbeard" gives its caster a magic silver beard that blocks attacks. The spell explicitly notes that the user grows a beard if lacking one (casters with an existing beard have theirs modified), even if they can't normally grow one (Listing Elf or female Human as examples).
    • In the earliest editions of its lore, due to being heavily based on Tolkien's dwarves, D&D dwarf women grew beards, although depending on the setting there might be changes to this — Greyhawk dwarf women having a cultural tendency to shave, Dragonlance dwarf women growing sideburns rather than beards, et. As the game aged, however, bearded dwarf women became more controversial and TSR began quietly sweeping that lore away. When Wizards of the Coast bought the license and released 3rd edition, they tossed the idea out; thus, Sharindlar, Dwarf Goddess of Love, went from having a massive flaming red beard in 2nd edition to being clean-shaven in 3rd. "Wizards Presents: Races & Monsters", a design preview book for 4th edition, even had a section explicitly referring to this trait and explaining why it had been officially removed from the setting, since it tended to do nothing but make dwarf women a bad joke and alienate female players from the dwarf race. 5e's release brings back optional female dwarves with beards, mentioned once as a possibility in the handbook.
    • There's also the Belt of Dwarvenkind which, aside from giving the wearer the Constitution, poison resistance and language skills of a dwarf, also gives you a fifty percent chance each dawn of growing a full beard, as long as you are capable of growing one. Depending on how the DM interprets that last rule, bearded women are certainly a possibility.
  • Pathfinder: Female manticores with human heads grow beards the same as males. Likewise, lion-headed females sport full manes.
  • Warhammer: Subverted in regards to the dwarfs. People do believe, in-universe, that dwarf women grow beards. They don't, and dwarfs find this belief highly insulting. In place of the men's elaborate beards, dwarf women instead wear their hair long, untrimmed and complexly braided.

    Real Life 
  • Having a beard as a woman can be a symptom of an actual medical condition known as "hirsutism" and is caused by a higher-than-normal level of male hormones, which itself can have a number of causes (some of them quite serious).
    • Other cases can be caused by side-effects of medications, as the story of Balpreet Kaur relates.
    • PCOS is also a major cause of this.
  • Many women with typical male hormone levels also have moustaches, albeit thin ones. It tends to be more obvious on those with naturally dark and/or thick hair.
  • Mexican painter Frida Kahlo was/is famous for her moustache, even though it was not very thick, as it was at that time considered unusual for a woman to not remove her beard.
  • Female rulers of Egypt (such as Cleopatra) used to wear the same royal attributes as the males when performing their official duties, which included a stylized fake beard.
  • In Brazil, it's a common joke about Portuguese women. The reasons are many: immigrants who really had them, the fact that most Portuguese men had them, and taking any opportunity to mock the former colonizers.
  • In 16th-century Poland, a Sensual Slav would have included a "moss" on her upper lip. While a full beard was seen as too masculine, just a little wisp of one was coyly feminine.
  • Naser al Din Shah, Qajar Shahanshah of Iran had a thing for girls with moustaches (he had a Badass Mustache of epic proportions himself). He was also a major Chubby Chaser and one of the first people in the world to acquire a photo camera, so every photo taken of women from his time was, well...mentioning Qajar-era beauty standards is an easy way to make an Iranian laugh.
  • A well known freakshow artist with a beard was Percilla Bejano, who is also namedropped in Tom Waits' song "Lucky Day Overture" from The Black Rider.
  • Some Drag Queens keep their beards (and other body hair) when performing if their aim is androgyny rather than female impersonation. In drag circles, they're known as "genderfuck" queens, the most famous example being Eurovision Song Contest winner Conchita Wurst.
  • Cats, walruses, and many other whiskered animals.
