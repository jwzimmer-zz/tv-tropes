Spiteful Spit

Go To

Edit Locked

  • Main
  • Laconic
  • Quotes
  • Create New

That's the good in him splattered on the glass.

"I spit in the face of people who do not want to be cool."
— Carlito Colón
Advertisement:

There are many ways to show how much contempt a person has for someone else. One classic way is through spitting. In general, spitting is considered a rather rude, disgusting and unsanitary habit. Spitting on another person is also a form of assault. When somebody is forced to wear that spit on their face? Well... that can be a bit insulting. This can be used by any character, whether they be the protagonist refusing to Kneel Before Zod, or the Defiant Captive's retort to I Have You Now, My Pretty, or the villain rejecting the hero's offer of a Last-Second Chance.

In fiction, Spiteful Spit usually shows up in three flavors:

  • When one person spits on another, usually the face.
    • A variation on this is spitting on someone's corpse, a sign of ultimate disrespect for that person (not to mention a lack of respect - see Due to the Dead).
  • Spitting on a person's prized possession. This version is more stealth, but still shows quite a bit of contempt.
    • Advertisement:
  • Spitting at the mere mention of the other person's name — which may be either a show of disrespect or, in some cultures, a way to ward off bad luck associated with a cursed or evil person.

How someone reacts to being on the receiving end of this trope can say quite a bit about them. If they lash out instantly, they may be Hot-Blooded or Not So Stoic. Alternately, a Consummate Professional or The Stoic might instead calmly wipe the spit off (if they were spat on directly) or simply ignore it.

Compare Second Face Smoke, Excrement Statement.

  • Rurouni Kenshin:
    • One of Shishio's Mooks gets captured by the Kyoto ninja, and spits in defiance when questioned. The head ninja's response is to tell his assistants to break out the long nails and candle wax.

He tells the mooks not to desecrate the graves, in response to which one of them contemptuously spits on one gravestone. Aoshi does not take it well.

Gokudera then dives into a fight with Gamma he definitely won't win. When he loses Gamma tortures him for information and Gokudera responds by spitting in his face.

    Religion 
  • In The Bible: In Deuteronomy 25:5-6, if a woman's husband dies and leaves her without children, and if the husband has an unmarried brother living nearby, then the brother must marry the husband's widow so that the first child he sires through her (ideally a son) will carry on the dead husband's name so that it will not be blotted out in Israel. If the brother decides that he's not going to marry her, verses 7 to 10 says that he must be brought before the elders, and if he declares that he still isn't going to marry her, then the widow may pull off his sandal, spit in his face, and say "So shall it be done to the man who will not build up his brother's house." And from that day on, his house will be called "the house of the unsandaled man."
  • The Book of Isaiah in traditional Christian interpretation prophesies that God's Suffering Servant, who is Jesus Christ, would be treated with shameful spitting, as He eventually does at His arrest, trial, and crucifixion.

    Real life 
  • The ancient Greek philosopher Diogenes was once invited to a rich man's house who told him not to spit on the ground, since there were expensive carpets and such everywhere. Diogenes spat in the man's face and said that he couldn't find another place. Yes, Diogenes was quite a punk.
  • In Israel and Jewish culture, as well as Muslim and Arabic-speaking countries, and Eastern Asia, spitting at someone is considered a highly insulting gesture and one of the worst taboos to be committed.
  • In Romania and Moldova, the former habit of gently spitting in the face of younger people was a sign of admiration to avoid accidentally cursing them; this is no longer widely practiced due to being unhygienic.
  • In northern India, mothers would lightly spit at their children to imply a sense of lighthearted disparagement and imperfection to protect their children from the evil eye and keep them humble, because excessive admiration could have the reverse effect of attracting the evil eye. This practice is declining due to health concerns, and a small mark of kohl or kajal is occasionally placed on the forehead or cheek to ward off the evil eye.
