The major cities of the world and their appearances in fiction. This page is based on the 2018 classifications and rankings of the Globalization and World Cities Research Network (GaWC) think tank group from Loughborough University in England, the earliest group to attempt such a categorization from way back in 1998. Expect some of these to show up in a World Tour.

Alpha++, Alpha+, Alpha and Alpha-

    Alpha++ 
  • London, England, United Kingdom: Largest city in Europe outside Russia and Turkey, and a center of finance, culture, politics etc. in the Old World, as well as a hotspot of diversity. It is also the most visited city in the world in terms of international arrivals and the first to host the Olympic Games thrice (1908, 1948 and 2012).
  • New York City, New York, United States: The Big Applesauce itself, the largest city in the United States, London's counterpart in the New World, and arguably the reigning cultural and financial capital of the world, as well as home of the General Assembly of the United Nations. Its position on one of the world's largest natural harbors made an excellent point-of-entry for traders and immigrants alike.
    Alpha+ 
  • Beijing, China: Capital of China for 800 years, second-largest city after Shanghai, and center of education, politics and culture. Best-known for its imperial complex, long closed to outsiders until the early 20th century.
  • Dubai, Emirate of Dubai, United Arab Emirates: Largest city of the UAE. Once a dusty little emirate capital, the discovery of oil propelled Dubai into a massive facelift, one that was briefly interrupted by the financial meltdown of 2008 but has since been making rebounds.
  • Hong Kong, China: The largest economic powerhouse in the Far East. Formerly a part of The British Empire until 1997, Hong Kong remains an enclave of capitalism in an otherwise communist state (an example followed by Macau two years later), while the British experience bequeathed the island-city with a unique culture and government system.
  • Paris, Île-de-France, France: Capital and largest city of France, and a global leader in culture and business. Its historical experiences also shaped the modern world through such events as The Enlightenment and The French Revolution. Today it is home to some of the most visited museums and landmarks in the world, as well as the second city to host the Olympics thrice (1900, 1924 and 2024).
  • Shanghai, China: Most populous city in China and its financial powerhouse. Strategically located at the mouth of the Yangtze River, Shanghai was among the first Chinese cities opened to foreign trade, enjoying a period of prosperity before going into a slump when trade was limited to other communist countries, then made a resurgent comeback during the Deng Xiaoping years.
  • Singapore: The powerhouse of Southeast Asia, the modern island-city-state of Singapore was founded by the British in 1819 as a trading post for the East India Company and later grew as a center of commerce and multiculturalism under a conservative democracy.
  • Sydney, New South Wales, Australia: The largest city in Oceania and the oldest colonial settlement in Australia, originally founded on a natural harbor as a penal colony before transforming into the premier financial center of the Asia Pacific region.
  • Tokyo, Japan: Arguably the center of the universe, capital and largest city of Japan, and center of the world's most populous metropolitan area (at 37.8 million), as well as a center of business and culture.
    Alpha 
  • Bangkok, Thailand: Capital and largest city of Thailand as well as the most visited city in Southeast Asia, as well as an economic power in the region's mainland half. It is well known for its street life, cultural landmarks, and its red-light district.
  • Brussels, Belgium: Capital and largest city of Belgium, directly located in between its two major cultural regions of Flanders and Wallonia, and the seat of many an international political organization, most prominent of which are the The European Union and NATO.
  • Buenos Aires, Argentina: Capital and largest city of Argentina and second-largest in South America. Known as the "Paris of the South", Buenos Aires is also known as a center of commerce and culture.
  • Chicago, Illinois, United States: Third-largest city in the USA and the urban heart of the Midwest. Built on the southwestern shores of Lake Michigan, the city grew into a major transportation hub, with more roads and railroads leading into it than any other city in America. Besides being a commercial center, it is also a heavyweight in American gastronomy and music.
  • Frankfurt [am Main], Hesse, Germany: The financial heartland of continental Europe, fifth-largest city of Germany, and home of the hot dog. It is also distinct from other German cities by its abundance of skyscrapers and autobahn interchanges.
  • Guangzhou, Guangdong, China: Provincial capital of Guangdong and third-largest city in China. The city grew into one of China's most important trade posts, and during the post-Mao years also became a financial hub.
  • Istanbul, Istanbul Province, Turkey: Largest city in Turkey, straddling Asia and Europe, divided by the Bosphorus strait. The city's long history as capital of four empires (Roman, Byzantine, Latin and Ottoman) and a major stopover of the Silk Road left a legacy of multiculturalism felt even until the present.
  • Jakarta, Jakarta Province, Indonesia: Capital and largest city of Indonesia, founded 1600 years ago as seat of the Sunda Kingdom and expanded its influence under the Dutch. Today it is a bustling metropolis of 9.6 million people, and kind of notorious of being dubbed as the city with the worst traffic jam in the world.
  • Kuala Lumpur, Malaysia: Capital and largest city of Malaysia (with nearby Putrajaya now serving as seat of the federal executive and judiciary, while the legislative and royal seats remain in this city). This little metropolis on the Klang Valley grew from a frontier town built by Chinese tin miners to the heart of the Malay Peninsula, culminating in an economic boom best symbolized by the Petronas Towers, the tallest twin buildings in the world.
  • Los Angeles, California, United States: Largest city in California and second-largest in the USA and a hotbed of multicultural diversity. The city is both a powerhouse of West Coast finance and as well as arguably the biggest television and film industry in the world, usually named after the neighborhood of Hollywood where some early major motion-picture companies were established, as well as a powerful sporting city, hosting several successful professional sports leagues as well as the third city to host the Olympics thrice (1932, 1984 and 2028).
  • Madrid, Autonomous Community of Madrid, Spain: Capital and largest city of Spain, as well as the geographic, political, economic and cultural center of the Iberian Peninsula.
  • Melbourne, Victoria, Australia: State capital of Victoria and second-largest city in Australia, and Sydney's long-time rival, as well as the first capital of Australia immediately upon independence. Melbourne is also Australia's cultural capital, being the birthplace of its local dance and film industries.
  • Mexico City, Mexico: Capital of Mexico, largest city in North America, and the oldest capital city in the New World, built by the Spanish over what was Tenochtitlan, seat of the Aztec Empire. Formerly a federal district like Washington, DC, but now a sui generis federal entity with the same powers as a state.
  • Miami, Florida, United States: The most populous metropolis in the southeastern USA and home to the largest concentration of banks in the Union and the second-largest Hispanophone majority (after El Paso, Texas), most of which were descended from Cubans fleeing its communist government.
  • Milan, Lombardy, Italy: Regional capital of Lombardy and second-largest city in Italy, as well as the financial and industrial heart of the north. Milan is also the world's fashion and design capital and is home to two of the world's most successful football clubs (AC Milan and FC Internazionale).
  • Moscow, Russia: Capital and largest city of Russia, as well as the northernmost and coldest metropolis in the world. Moscow is, for centuries, both the heart of Russian culture and politics, from the imperial to the communist and into the federal periods.
  • Mumbai (Bombay), Maharashtra, India: The "Pearl of the Arabian Sea", the largest city in India and its unofficial second capital. Patched together from seven islands by a British reclamation project, Mumbai grew into the financial heart of Southern Asia, and is also the center of India's largest entertainment industry.
  • São Paulo, São Paulo State, Brazil: Largest city in the Americas, a financial powerhouse in South America, and land of capricious weather. It is famous for its culinary delights, the largest fleet of helicopters in the world, and its insane traffic jams.note Said traffic jams are the main reason the city has such a huge fleet of choppers—they're literally the only way high-powered businesspeople can move within the city. It's gotten to the point that helicopter taxis now serve the city.
  • Seoul, Seoul National Capital Area, South Korea: The 2,000-year-old heart of the Korean Peninsula and capital and largest city of the Republic of Korea (South Korea). Following the devastation of the Korean War, Seoul experienced a massive economic boom, culminating in hosting the 1988 Summer Olympics, and is also the birthplace of the Korean music craze.
  • Taipei, Taiwan: Capital and largest city of the Republic of China (Taiwan) and the beating financial and cultural heart of the island.
  • Toronto, Ontario, Canada: The largest city of Canada and a hotspot of ethnic diversity, with almost half of its 2.6 million residents coming from outside Canada.
  • Warsaw, Masovian Voivodeship, Poland: Capital and largest city of Poland, majority of which was built from scratch after it was near-totally flattened by the Nazis during World War II. Much of the city's income comes from heavy industries, a legacy of Soviet influence.
  • Zürich, Canton of Zürich, Switzerland: Largest city in Switzerland, established by the Romans over what used to be a group of prehistoric settlements. It is also home to many of the world's largest banking institutions.
    Alpha- 
  • Amsterdam, North Holland, The Netherlands: Capital and largest city of the Netherlands, as well as its commercial and cultural hub, famous for its canal-lined city center and a very liberal atmosphere.
  • Barcelona, Catalonia, Spain: State capital of Catalonia and second-largest city in Spain, famous for its avant-garde architecture and hosting the memorable 1992 Summer Olympics.
  • Bogotá, Distrito Capital, Colombia: Capital and largest city of Colombia, known as the "Athens of South America" for its abundance of universities.
  • Budapest, Hungary: Capital and largest city of Hungary, being a regional capital since Roman times. It is also one of the most beautiful cities in Europe, with its dramatic setting on the Danube River, as well as host to several hot springs.
  • Dublin, Ireland: Capital of the Republic of Ireland and largest city in the entire island, as well as a center of education, arts, commerce and industry.
  • Houston, Texas, United States: Largest city in Texas, largely fueled by its several railroad and ship connections, further bolstered by the discovery of oil. It is also home to NASA's control center.
  • Johannesburg, Gauteng, South Africa: Largest city in South Africa and the economic powerhouse of Sub-Saharan Africa, built near some of the largest gold and diamond mines in the world.
  • Lisbon, Lisbon District, Portugal: Capital and largest city of Portugal, situated on the northern side of the mouth of the Tagus River, as well as the oldest capital city in Western Europe.
  • Luxembourg City, Luxembourg District, Luxembourg: Capital and largest city of the tiny Grand Duchy of Luxembourg, as well as the financial seat of both the country and the European Union.
  • Manila, Philippines: Capital and largest city of the Philippines, located at the crossroads of several Pacific trade routes. It is actually composed of sixteen cities and one municipality, all centered around the eponymous Spanish-era city, said to be the most densely-populated in the world.
  • Montreal, Quebec, Canada: Second-largest city of Canada and second-largest Francophone city in the world after Paris, as well as, until The '70s, the chief commercial center of the country.
  • Munich, Bavaria, Germany: State capital of Bavaria and third-largest city in Germany. Famous for its architecture, a highly-successful football team (Bayern Munich FC) and the original (and largest) Oktoberfest.
  • New Delhi, National Capital Territory, India Capital of India and its largest metropolitan region, built near the historic capital of several Indian empires.
  • Prague, Czech Republic: Capital and largest city of the Czech Republic. The city waxed and waned together with the Holy Roman Empire, and the city center largely escaped the ravages of World War II, which makes for good sightseeing. And filming.
  • Riyadh, Riyadh Region, Saudi Arabia: Capital and largest city of Saudi Arabia and second-largest in the Arab World. Despite its location in the middle of the desert, Riyadh evolved from a trade post by way of being the ancestral home of the kingdom's ruling family.
  • Rome, Lazio, Italy: The "Eternal City", capital and largest city of Italy. For centuries it served as the beating heart of the Roman Empire, and is currently the seat of both the Italian government and the Roman Catholic Church, within the enclave of Vatican City, a remnant of The Papal States that once controlled central Italy.
  • San Francisco, California, United States: The cultural and financial hub of northern California, located at the mouth of the Golden Gate. The "City by the Bay" is well-known for its streetcars and its liberal atmosphere, with a history of hippie counterculture and LGBT activism.
  • Santiago, Santiago Metropolitan Region, Chile: Capital and largest city of Chile, known for a well-preserved city center and the vineyards located in its outskirts.
  • Shenzhen, Guangdong, China: Second-largest city of Guangzhou, established as China's first Special Economic Zone during The '70s (and directly to the north of the then-British territory of Hong Kong) in its efforts to open up to the world during the Deng Xiaoping years.
  • Stockholm, Södermanland/Uppland, Sweden: Capital of Sweden and the largest city in Nordic Europe. Stockholm is the home to Swedish culture, media and economy, and is the venue for the Nobel Prize awarding ceremonies (save the Peace Prize, which is handed in Oslo).
  • Vienna, Austria: Capital and largest city of Austria, home to a fifth of the country's population, and second-largest Germanophone city in the world after Berlin. The city, also one of Austria's nine states, is best-known for its palaces and centuries of musical heritage.
  • Washington, District of Columbia, United States: Capital of the United States, established in 1791 on the banks of the Potomac River. Besides serving as home to the USA's three branches of government, Washington also plays host to several other international organizations, and boasts a wide array of landmarks, many of which are located on the National Mall, an axis of greenery from the eastern banks of the Potomac to Capitol Hill.

Beta+, Beta and Beta-

    Beta+ 
  • Athens, Attica, Greece: Capital and largest city of Greece, as well as the birthplace of democracy and classical culture, as well as host to the largest passenger port in Europe in nearby Piraeus. It is also a historic city, with such landmarks as the Parthenon (temple to Athena, the city's namesake patron goddess), and hosted both the inaugural Olympic Games in 1896 and its homecoming in 2004.
  • Atlanta, Georgia, United States: State capital and largest city of Georgia, as well as the unofficial capital of the "New South", being a center of racial diversity (as well as a hotspot of the Civil Rights Movement, being the hometown of Martin Luther King Jr., who also served as a local Baptist pastor for the last years of his life), a liberal hotspot in the otherwise conservative region, and an economic boomtown, spurred on by hosting the centenary Olympic Games in 1996. Home to the world's busiest airport.
  • Auckland, Auckland Region, New Zealand: Largest city of New Zealand as well as the largest Polynesian city, as well as a favorite destination for East Asian migrants.
  • Bangalore (Bengaluru), Karnataka, India: State capital of Karnataka, dubbed the "Silicon Valley of India" for its leading role in India's IT industry.
  • Boston, Massachusetts, United States: State capital of Massachusetts and largest city in the New England region. Boston is also one of the oldest colonial-era settlements in America, played a prominent role in the American Revolution, and is also home to several prestigious educational institutions, including Harvard University, the oldest institute of higher learning in America.
  • Bucharest, Romania: The "Little Paris" of Eastern Europe, capital and largest city of Romania. The city is notable for its eclectic mix of classical, socialist and modern architectural styles at the city center.
  • Cairo, Cairo Governorate, Egypt: Capital of Egypt and largest city in Northern Africa and the Arab World. Cairo is a center of Islamic learning and worship, and is also the oldest entertainment exporter in the Arab world, as well as home to the Pyramids and Sphinx of the Giza Plateau to the southwest, among the oldest manmade structures in the world.
  • Chengdu, Sichuan, China: Provincial capital of Sichuan and largest city in western China, as well as the region's historic, economic and cultural center.
  • Copenhagen, Hovedstaden, Denmark: Capital and largest city of Denmark, as well as the second largest city in Nordic Europe and the cultural, political and economic center of the country.
  • Dallas, Texas, United States: Third-largest city in Texas, having grown on the back of its cotton and oil industries. Together with the slightly smaller city of Fort Worth and a few other cities and towns, Dallas forms the largest urban area in the South, and one of the few major conservative-leaning urban areas.
  • Doha, Qatar: Capital and largest city of Qatar. Besides being a center of government, Doha is also a bustling economic and educational giant in the Persian Gulf.
  • Düsseldorf, North Rhine-Westphalia, Germany:: State capital of North Rhine-Westphalia and center of the Ruhr Valley metropolitan area, which was the heart of the German industrial renaissance, now a center of the arts.
  • Hamburg, Germany: Second-largest city in Germany and a city-state in its own right, as well as a mercantile power back in the days of the Hanseatic League and still home to the largest port in Germany.
  • Hangzhou, Zhejiang, China: Provincial capital of Zhejiang and a historically important center, being the easternmost end of the Grand Canal which once served as the major maritime route to Beijing and is still the longest artificial waterway in the world.
  • Hanoi, Vietnam: Capital and second-largest city of Vietnam, as well as the political heart of the region for a thousand years, and features a mix of ancient and modern lifestyles.
  • Ho Chi Minh City, Vietnam: Largest city of Vietnam, known as Saigon until after The Vietnam War. The city thrives both as a Khmer and French port city, which continues even after the emergence of Hanoi to the north.
  • Lima, Peru: Capital and largest city in Peru, founded by the Spanish to also serve as capital of the Viceroyalty of Peru. It is also home to the National University of San Marcos, the oldest functioning university in the New World.
  • Perth, Western Australia, Australia: State capital and largest city, as well as reputedly one of the most isolated cities in the world. Despite its location, the city thrives largely on the state's mining industry.
  • Tel Aviv, Tel Aviv District, Israel: Second-largest city of Israel as well as its economic center, boasting the largest Middle Eastern economy outside Dubai, as well as a bastion of liberal politics and culture.
  • Vancouver, British Columbia, Canada: Provincial capital of British Columbia and largest city in western Canada, which thrived both as a port city and a center of forestry and tourism, as well as one of the largest film production centers in North America. Its dramatic location between the sea and the Pacific Cascades mountains also made it the host of the 2010 Winter Olympics.
    Beta 
  • Abu Dhabi, Emirate of Abu Dhabi, United Arab Emirates: Capital and second-largest city of the UAE. Once a small fishing village, the discovery of oil propelled Abu Dhabi to unprecedented riches, but nevertheless has a more laid-back atmosphere than Dubai to the east.
  • Beirut, Beirut Governorate, Lebanon: Capital and largest city of Lebanon. Built on the Mediterranean shore, Beirut once thrived as a major seaport until it was devastated during the Lebanese Civil War, but has since rebounded and regained its prosperity.
  • Berlin, Germany: Capital and largest city of Germany, and like Hamburg a city-state in its own right. Long the seat of several empires, the city fell into ruin at the end of World War II and later literally divided by a concrete wall throughout the Cold War, but after the fall of the wall in 1989 and reunification a year later, rebounded as a center of culture, politics, media and science.
  • Brisbane, Queensland, Australia: State capital and largest city of northeastern Australia, as well as the third-largest nationwide. Besides playing a role as an Allied Forces HQ during World War II, Brisbane is otherwise a pretty peaceful metropolis.
  • Calgary, Alberta, Canada: Largest city of Alberta and a business giant in central Canada, as well as home to the Calgary Stampede, one of the world's largest rodeo festivals and the self-proclaimed "Greatest Outdoor Show on Earth".
  • Cape Town, Western Cape, South Africa: Second-largest city in South Africa and the legislative capital. Situated at the Cape Bay, the city was the very first European settlement in Southern Africa, and later grew into a multicultural haven.
  • Caracas, Venezuelan Capital District, Venezuela: Capital and largest city of Venezuela, thriving largely on the country's oil industry.
  • Casablanca, Grand Casablanca, Morocco: Largest city of Morocco and the nation's economic and cultural heart, as well as the home to the largest port in northern Africa and the setting of an unforgettable 1940s romance film.
  • Chennai (Madras), Tamil Nadu, India: State capital of Tamil Nadu, largest city of southern India, fourth-largest throughout India, and a thriving center of the automotive and health industries, as well as a hotspot of ethnic diversity.
  • Denver, Colorado, United States: State capital and largest city of Colorado, sitting roughly at one mile (1.609 kilometers) above sea level (thus the nickname "Mile High City"). Founded as a gold rush town, Denver continued to flourish long after the mines dried up as an important crossroads between the Midwest and the Rockies.
  • Karachi, Sindh, Pakistan: State capital of Sindh and largest city of Pakistan, as well as its economic center and erstwhile national capital. Despite the government's move inland, Karachi remains one of South Asia's largest economies, and is one of the most diverse places in Pakistan.
  • Kiev, Ukraine: Capital and largest city of Ukraine, as well as the heartland of Ukrainian culture since Russian times. The city is also the most pro-European part of the deeply politically-divided country.
  • Kuwait City, Al Asima Governorate, Kuwait: Capital and largest city of Kuwait. Long a prosperous trading post between the Middle East and southern Asia, Kuwait City experienced a massive surge of relevance after the discovery of oil, and continues to thrive even after suffering the brunt of the Gulf War.
  • Lagos, Lagos State, Nigeria: Largest city of Nigeria and all of Africa, as well as a thriving business hub, having largely prospered on the back of the country's oil industry, and a center of the arts.
  • Manama, Capital Governorate, Bahrain: Capital and largest city of the island-state of Bahrain, as well as a strategic financial center, being located at the middle of the Persian Gulf.
  • Minneapolis, Minnesota, United States: Largest city of Minnesota and, together with the smaller state capital Saint Paul, the fourteenth-largest metropolis in America and largest on the Midwestern prairie. Once the world's largest flour manufacturer, the Twin Cities continues to thrive as a lumber center and a trade post between Seattle and Chicago.
  • Montevideo, Montevideo Department, Uruguay: Capital and largest city of Uruguay, established on the northeastern banks of the Río de la Plata as a Spanish fort amidst the Spanish-Portuguese race for lands in South America.
  • Nairobi, Nairobi County, Kenya: Capital of Kenya and largest city in eastern Africa, founded by the British as a trading post between Uganda and the Indian Ocean coast.
  • Nanjing, Jiangsu, China: Provincial capital of Jiangsu. Seated on the banks of the mighty Yangtze River, Nanjing has, as late as 1949, served as imperial and republican capital for several centuries, and is a major educational center in modern China.
  • Oslo, Oslo County, Norway: Capital and largest city of Norway, as well as the fastest-growing in Europe. Besides being a center of government and commerce, Oslo is also a major maritime power, as well as a center of arts and tourism.
  • Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, United States: Largest city in Pennsylvania, a center of arts and culture, and once the largest city in America by the 1770s. The city also played a role in the American Revolution, when American independence was signed there on July 4, 1776.
  • Rio de Janeiro, Rio de Janeiro State, Brazil: Second-largest city of Brazil and the nation's undisputed cultural and tourist capital, as well as the long-time capital of Brazil before the government moved to centrally-located, purpose-built Brasília. Famed for its beaches, an iconic statue of Jesus with his arms outstretched, its samba and carnival cultures, and a major sporting city in South America, culminating in it hosting the first Olympic Games in the continent in 2016.
  • Sofia, Bulgaria: Capital and largest city of Bulgaria. Founded by Thracians some 3,000 years ago, the city thrived under the Romans as regional center, and has since slipped into quietude until it was reestablished as national capital in 1879.
  • Tianjin, China: Fourth-largest city in China and the chief port of entry for maritime trade going into Beijing.
  • Wuhan, Hubei, China: Provincial capital of Hubei and largest city in central China. Gained international notoriety in the early 2020s as the city where a deadly, fast-spreading strain of the coronavirus was first identified, and which has since become a global pandemic.
  • Zagreb, Croatia: Capital and largest city of Croatia. Founded in the Roman era, the city flourished throughout the medieval period, and bloomed into a powerful city in the 19th century.
    Beta- 
  • Almaty, Kazakhstan: Largest city of Kazakhstan and former capital until the government moved inward to more geographically-centralized Astana (later renamed Nur-Sultan). Despite this, Almaty remained the country's leading economic and cultural hub.
  • Antwerp, Flanders, Belgium: Second-largest city of Belgium and de facto capital of the Dutch-speaking Flanders region, as well as home to the country's largest port.
  • Belgrade, Serbia: Capital and largest city of Serbia. Strategically located between the Pannonian Plain and the Balkan Peninsula, and sitting on the confluence of the Danube and Sava Rivers, it features a unique combination of Slavic, Ottoman, socialist and modern sensibilities.
  • Birmingham, England, United Kingdom: Second-largest city of the United Kingdom and an industrial powerhouse, having tried for almost every trade available at the time of the Industrial Revolution, before turning its attention to the services sector during the last quarter of the 20th century.
  • Bratislava, Bratislava Region, Slovakia: Capital and largest city of Slovakia. Besides being a political, financial and cultural hub, Bratislava for centuries was the crossroads of many empires and races.
  • Changsha, Hunan, China: Provincial capital and largest city of Hunan and an important industrial as well as historic city, famous for being the city where Mao Zedong started his political career.
  • Chongqing, China: One of China's four directly-administered municipalities (the others being Beijing, Shanghai and Tianjin) and a historic center of economy in southwest China as well as the upstream region of the Yangtze River.
  • Dalian, Liaoning, China: Largest city of Liaoning and a major seaport for northeastern Asia, having seen a history of foreign trade and occupation.
  • Dhaka, Dhaka Division, Bangladesh: Capital and largest city of Bangladesh, as well as the fourth most densely-populated city in the world and a microcosm of the diversity of the entire nation.
  • Edinburgh, Scotland, United Kingdom: Capital and second-largest city of Scotland, as well as a noted educational center with a well-preserved city center.
  • Geneva, Canton of Geneva, Switzerland: Second-largest city of Switzerland, situated on the shores of the same-named lake, known for its thriving businesses and being the home of several branches of the United Nations and The Red Cross.
  • George Town, Cayman Islands: Capital of the Cayman Islands and largest city of the British Overseas Territories, and center of the islands' status as offshore financial havens.
  • Helsinki, Uusimaa, Finland: Capital and largest city of Finland, as well as the country's center of government, culture and finance.
  • Jeddah, Makkah Region, Saudi Arabia: Second-largest city of Saudi Arabia, as well as the largest port in the Red Sea and the principal gateway to Mecca (Makkah), the Holy City of Islam 70 kilometers (43 miles) to the east, as well as Medina, the second-holiest city.
  • Jinan, Shandong, China: Provincial capital of Shandong and one of the oldest cities in China, famed for its dozens of artesian springs.
  • Kampala, Kampala District, Uganda: Capital and largest city of Uganda and a rapidly-developing powerhouse in East Africa.
  • Lyon, Auvergne-Rhône-Alpes, France: Regional capital of Auvergne-Rhône-Alpes and second-largest city of France. Lyon is known for its well-preserved city center, a once-thriving silk industry, and home to some of France's best dining scenes.
  • Manchester, England, United Kingdom: Second largest city of England and the United Kingdom. The city suddenly grew in prominence during the Industrial Revolution, and even long after the industries have gone, it thrives as a center of culture and business, as well as home to two of England's most successful football teams (historically prominent Manchester United and 2010s powerhouse Manchester City).
  • Monterrey, Nuevo León, Mexico: State capital of Nuevo León and third-largest city of Mexico, as well as the richest and most Americanized in the northern half of the country.
  • Nicosia (Lefkosia), Cyprus: Capital and largest city of Cyprus, bordered by a series of walls that exist to this day and still divided into Greek and Turkish sectors, a legacy of the two countries' feud.
  • Panama City, Panamá District, Panama: Capital and largest city of Panama as well as the most cosmopolitan in Central America. Founded by the Spanish as the Pacific end of an inland trade route, the city prospered even further with the construction of the Panama Canal.
  • Port Louis, Port Louis District, Mauritius: Capital and largest city of Mauritius, as well as a mercantile center and the financial heart of southeastern Africa.
  • Qingdao, Shandong, China: Largest city of Shandong and a major seaport, as well as home to the revered Tsingtao beer created by German immigrants in the early 20th century.
  • Quito, Pichincha, Ecuador: Capital and second-largest city of Ecuador, as well as the highest capital in the world. Besides being a financial and political hub, the city boasts one of the best-preserved Spanish-era cities in the world.
  • San José, San José Province, Costa Rica: Capital and largest city of Costa Rica, as well as one of the most European-like cities in Central America.
  • San Juan, Puerto Rico: Capital and largest city of Puerto Rico and the second-oldest colonial settlement in the New World (after Santo Domingo), and home to half of the island's population.
  • San Salvador, San Salvador Department, El Salvador: Capital and largest city of El Salvador and a major Central American financial center.
  • Seattle, Washington, United States: Largest city of Washington state. Founded in the 1850s, Seattle made brief trysts with fame as the main stop for the gold rush of the 1890s and host of the 1962 World's Fair, before making a full bloom as home of grunge rock and such global corporate icons as Boeing, Microsoft, Starbucks and Amazon.
  • Shenyang, Liaoning, China: Provincial capital of Liaoning and largest city in northeastern China, as well as a historic seat of the Manchu-dominated Qin dynasty.
  • Stuttgart, Baden-Württemberg, Germany: State capital of Baden-Württemberg and sixth-largest city in Germany, as well as the heart of the Swabian peoples of southwestern Germany.
  • Suzhou, Jiangsu, China: Second-largest city of Jiangsu, lying directly between Nanjing and Shanghai on the southern banks of the Yangtze River, and a historic and economic powerhouse.
  • Tunis, Tunis Governorate, Tunisia: Capital and largest city of Tunisia, built near where the once-mighty city of Carthage stood.

  • Kazan, Tatarstan, Russia: Capital of the Republic of Tatarstan, home to the Volga Tatars, the largest ethnic minority group in Russia, and sixth-largest city of Russia, as well as its self-proclaimed "third capital", famed for being an intersection of European and Asian influences.
  • Kigali, Rwanda: Capital and largest city of Rwanda. Founded in the early 20th century by Belgian colonists, the city grew as a regional hub and has been undergoing recovery and development following a terrible civil war and genocide in the 1990s.
  • Kingston, Kingston & Saint Andrew Parishes, Jamaica: Capital and largest city of Jamaica and a hotbed of the island-nation's famed musical culture.
  • Kinshasa, Democratic Republic of the Congo: Capital and largest city of the DR Congo, third-largest in Africa, and largest Francophone city in the world (surpassing even Paris).
  • Kochi (Cochin), Kerala, India: Largest city of Kerala and a major trading and naval port-of-call since the 14th century.
  • Kraków, Lesser Poland Voivodeship, Poland: Regional capital of Lesser Poland, second-largest city of Poland and one of its oldest as well as its former capital, originating as a hilltop hamlet and best known for its historic city center and the center of ministry of a certain Archbishop Karol Cardinal Wojtyła before he became Pope (now Saint) John Paul II.
  • Lanzhou, Gansu, China: Provincial capital and largest city of Gansu. Historically a link city of the Silk Road, currently it is a major player in China's heavy and petrochemical industries.

  • Leipzig, Saxony, Germany: A trade city since the age of the Holy Roman Empire, Leipzig once flourished as a center of music and culture in Saxony, and late in the 20th century made headlines as the origin of the peaceful demonstrations that accelerated the fall of East Germany.
  • León, Guanajuato, Mexico: Largest city of Guanajuato and seventh-largest in Mexico, famed for its leatherworks industry.
  • Libreville, Estuaire, Gabon: Capital and largest city of Gabon, as well as former chief port of French Equatorial Africa.
  • Liège, Wallonia, Belgium: Third-largest city of Belgium and largest city of the French-speaking Wallonia region.
  • Lille, Hauts-de-France, France: Regional capital of Hauts-de-France and northernmost major city in France, as well as a historic seat of the French Flanders region.
  • Limassol, Limassol District, Cyprus: Second-largest city of Cyprus and a major tourist destination.
  • Linz, Upper Austria, Austria: State capital of Upper Austria and third-largest city in Austria, with a history of major contributions to the arts and is curently a technological powerhouse.
  • Liverpool, England, United Kingdom: Fifth-largest metropolitan area in the UK and a historically important seaport, having seen off millions of British and Irish emigrants to the United States in the 19th century, as well as a major exporter of British music epitomized by The Beatles.
  • Łódź, Łódź Voivodeship, Poland: Capital of the same-named region and third-largest city in Poland, as well as a giant in the textiles industry before reinventing itself as a center of the arts.
  • Louisville, Kentucky, United States: More than just the Kentucky Derby and bourbon, Kentucky's largest city has developed a quite diversified economy, with health care and transportation being especially prominent nowadays.
  • Macau, China: A former Portuguese colony handed over to China in 1999, it's now a major resort area, as well as the world's leading gambling center, supplanting Las Vegas in that role. Notably the only location in China where casino gambling is legal.
  • Madison, Wisconsin, United States: State capital of Wisconsin; basically a miniature version of Columbus, Ohio in that it's a midwestern state capital that's also home to its state's largest university; the two universities are even members of the same sports conference.
  • Málaga, Andalusia, Spain: Second-largest city of Andalusia and a hub of the Costa del Sol, a coastal tourist destination, and birthplace of Pablo Picasso.
  • Malmö, Scania, Sweden: Regional capital and largest city of Scania and third-largest in Sweden, as well as a and a hotspot of diversity, symbolized by a bridge connecting Sweden and Denmark. It is also home to the tallest skyscraper in the Nordic countries.
  • Marseille, Provence-Alpes-Côte d'Azur, France: Regional capital of Provence-Alpes-Côte d'Azur, second-largest city in France and third-largest urban area (after Paris and Lyon). Marseille once thrived as the main trade post of France, and was also the birthplace of the French National Anthem, named in its honor.
  • Memphis, Tennessee, United States: Second-largest city of Tennesse and the largest along the Mississippi River, famed as the launching pad for many American musicians, such as Elvis Presley, Johnny Cash and Justin Timberlake.
  • Mérida, Yucatán, Mexico: State capital and largest city of Yucatán and the city with the largest proportion of indigenous peoples in Mexico, with the Maya still making up 60% of the total population.
  • Mexicali, Baja California, Mexico: State capital and second-largest city of Baja California, located on the border with California, and home to dozens of maquiladoras (duty-free factories for goods sold in the USA).
  • Montpellier, Occitanie, France: Second-largest city of Occitanie and a prosperous university city.
  • Nagoya, Aichi Prefecture, Japan: Prefectural capital of Aichi, fourth-largest city of Japan and largest of the Chūbu region and a major manufacturing city, among whose most famous products are the Toyota automobiles.
  • Nanchang, Jiangxi, China: Provincial capital and largest city of Jiangxi, famed as the birthplace of the People's Liberation Army.
  • Nanning, Guangxi, China: Provincial capital and largest city of Guangxi, an autonomous province designated as the home of the Zhuang ethnic minority.
  • Nantong, Jiangsu, China: Sixth-largest city of Jiangsu and a historic port city on the northern banks of the Yangtze River.
  • Naples, Campania, Italy: Regional capital of Campania and third-largest city in Italy, and the capital of the now-defunct Kingdom of the Two Sicilies that existed before Italian unification, known for its massive shipping harbors, their trademark "canzone napoletana", the first train station in Italy, and obviously, being the birthplace of pizza.
  • New Orleans, Louisiana, United States: Largest city of Louisiana, renowned for its multicultural (primarily French) heritage and easygoing life despite being in a state of continuous recovery following the devastation brought by Hurricane Katrina in 2005.
  • Newcastle upon Tyne, England, United Kingdom: A university city, most populous in northeastern England, and a major seaport.
  • Nice, Provence-Alpes-Côte d'Azur, France: Second-largest city of Provence-Alpes-Côte d'Azur and seventh-largest in France and a famously picturesque coastal city.
  • Nottingham, England, United Kingdom: Largest urban area in the East Midlands of England, famed as the center of activity of mythical bandit and folk hero Robin Hood, as well as home to several sports centers in England.
  • Novosibirsk, Novosibirsk Oblast, Russia: Capital and largest city of the same-named oblast, third-largest city in Russia, and largest city in Siberia.
  • Nuremberg, Bavaria, Germany: Second-largest city of Bavaria and fourteenth-largest in Germany, as well as the unofficial capital of the Franconia region, famous for its market but also infamous for being the birthplace of the Nazis as well as host to war crimes trials at the end of World War II.
  • Nur-Sultan (Astana), Kazakhstan: Capital and second-largest city of Kazakhstan, founded as "Akmola" in 1830 before being rebuilt in the late 1990s to serve as the country's new, geographically central capital.
  • Oklahoma City, Oklahoma, United States: Capital and largest city of the same-named state and home to one of the world's largest livestock markets, as well as a hub for the American petroleum industry.
  • Palo Alto, California, United States: Part of the San Francisco Bay Area, mostly famous for Stanford University and the high-tech and venture capital businesses it helped to spawn as part of the Silicon Valley region.
  • Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania, United States: Second-largest city of Pennsylvania and largest in the Ohio Valley, famous for its once-massive steelworks industry. After the decline of the industry in the 1970s and 1980s, it has reinvented itself as a medical, educational, and technological hub.
  • Podgorica, Podgorica Municipality, Montenegro: Capital and largest city of Montenegro and the economic, cultural and educational focus of the country.
  • Port Elizabeth, Eastern Cape, South Africa: Largest city of Eastern Cape and sixth-largest in South Africa and a gateway city for industries and tourism.
  • Port Harcourt, Rivers State, Nigeria: Capital and largest city of River State, which grew on the back of oil deposits discovered during the 1950s.
  • Port of Spain, Trinidad and Tobago: Capital of Trinidad and Tobago and home to the Caribbean islands' largest stock market.
  • Portland, Oregon, United States: Largest city of Oregon and a pocket of liberal politics in an otherwise conservative state (unlike Austin it's dominant enough to swing statewide elections Democratic), being a center of cultural diversity and a hotspot of outdoor activities.
  • Pretoria, Gauteng, South Africa: Executive capital of South Africa and a major center for education in the country.
  • Puebla City, Puebla, Mexico: State capital and largest city of Puebla and one of the most important business cities in central Mexico.
  • Quebec City, Quebec, Canada: The provincial capital is a significant business center as well as a major tourist attraction, thanks to its historic city center. Also the site of a key battle in the Seven Years' War that led to British control over all of Canada.
  • Recife, Pernambuco, Brazil: State capital and largest city of Pernambuco, as well as the largest city in northeastern Brazil and a cultural powerhouse.
  • Reykjavík, Capital Region, Iceland: Capital and largest city of Iceland and the northernmost national capital in the world.
  • Richmond, Virginia, United States: State capital of Virginia, which played a role in both the American Revolution and the Civil War.
  • Rochester, New York, United States: Third-largest city of New York and one of the first boomtowns of the USA, as well as the birthplace of several socio-political movements such as those for the abolition of slavery and adovcacy of women's rights.
  • Rosario, Santa Fe, Argentina: Largest city of the province of Santa Fe and thirdt-largest in Argentina and a major fluvial port city.
  • Salt Lake City, Utah, United States: State capital and largest city of Utah, famous as the worldwide headquarters of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-Day Saints (Mormons), as well as the industrial banking center of the USA.
  • San Luis Potosí City, San Luis Potosí, Mexico: Capital and largest city of the same-named state, originally founded on the back of silver and gold mining in nearby towns, and is currently a prolific manufacturing center.
  • San Pedro Sula, Cortés Department, Honduras: Departmental capital of Cortés and second-largest city in Honduras, but by far the country's main economic center.
  • Sanaa, Sanaa Governorate, Yemen: Largest city of Yemen and erstwhile capital before it was temporarily moved to Aden in 2015 due to political troubles.
  • Santa Cruz de la Sierra, Santa Cruz Department, Bolivia: Departmental capital of Santa Cruz and largest city in Bolivia as well as its principal business center.
  • Sarajevo, Bosnia and Herzegovina: Capital and largest city of Bosnia and Herzegovina, and a hotspot of cultural and religious diversity in the Balkans, which saw itself at the center of many historical events, from the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria which helped trigger World War I to being devastated during The Yugoslav Wars, from which it quickly recovered.
  • Seville, Andalusia, Spain: State capital and largest city of Andalusia and fourth-largest in Spain, as well as its only inland port and a former economic power during its imperial days, having been the starting point of an expedition led by Ferdinand Magellan to find a westward route to Asia in 1519, and to which it returned after three years (and a year after Magellan died in the Philippines) to complete the first recorded circumnavigation of the world.
  • Shijiazhuang, Hebei, China: Provincial capital and largest city of China, and twelfth-largest city in China and a major industrial center.
  • Strasbourg, Grand-Est, France: Regional capital and largest city of Grand-Est, ninth-largest city in France, as well as de facto capital of the historic Alsace region which, with nearby Lorraine (also currently in Grand-Est) constantly exchanged hands between France and Germany for centuries, and is currently home to the European Parliament.
  • Taichung, Taiwan: Second-largest city of Taiwan island and a major cultural center.
  • Tashkent, Uzbekistan: Capital and largest city of Uzbekistan, as well as the most populous in Central Asia and one of the most diverse, encouraged by its location along the Silk Road.
  • Tijuana, Baja California, Mexico: Largest city of Baja California as well as the sixth-largest and westernmost city of Mexico, lying just south of the border with the United States.
  • Toulouse, Occitanie, France: Regional capital and largest city of Occitanie and fourth-largest city in France, as well as home to one of the oldest universities in Europe and center of the European aerospace industry.
  • Trieste, Friuli-Venezia Giulia, Italy: Regional capital and largest city of Friuli-Venezia Giulia and a major port along the Adriatic Sea. For centuries the principal port city of the Austro-Hungarian Empire, it currently enjoys prestige as a hotspot of diversity and education.
  • Tulsa, Oklahoma, United States: Second-largest city of Oklahoma and a former oil boomtown before diversifying its economy and becoming the cultural center of the state.
  • Ulaanbaatar (Ulan Bator), Mongolia: Capital and largest city of Mongolia, and a major crossroads between Russia and China.
  • Ürümqi, Xinjiang, China: Provincial capital and largest city of Xinjiang, the designated home of the Uyghurs, a Muslim-majority Turkic people, and a historically strategic trade city along the Silk Road.
  • Utrecht, Utrecht Province, Netherlands: Capital and largest city of the same-named province and fourth-largest in the Netherlands, once the most important city in medieval Netherlands and remains the religious capital of the nation.
  • Valencia, Carabobo, Venezuela: State capital of Carabobo and third-largest city of Venezuela, and home to the nation's top industries and manufacturers.
  • Valparaíso, Valparaíso Region, Chile: Capital of the same-named region and second-largest city of Chile, as well as its principal seaport and home of the national legislature, and a powehouse of the region, being the home to Latin America's first stock exchange and the longest-running Spanish-language newspaper in the world.
  • Vientiane, Vientiane Prefecture, Laos: Capital and largest city of the Lao People's Democratic Republic, lying on the northern banks of the Mekong River and near the boundary with eastern Thailand.
  • Weifang, Shandong, China: Sixth-largest city of Shandong and a historic city known as the birthplace of kites.
  • Windhoek, Khomas Region, Namibia: Capital and largest city of Namibia and a center of economic activity in the country.
  • Winnipeg, Manitoba, Canada: Provincial capital and largest city of Manitoba and seventh-largest in Canada, as well as a historical trading post.
  • Wuxi, Jiangsu, China: Third-largest city of Jiangsu and a historic economic center which once exported rice, silk and textiles, and is currently a major technological center.
  • Xining, Qinghai, China: Provincial capital of Qinghai and largest city on the Tibetan Plateau, as well as a cultural and commercial hub along the northern sector of the Silk Road and gateway to the cultures of western China such as the Tibetans and Uyghurs.
  • Yerevan, Armenia: Capital and largest city of Armenia, and one of the oldest cities in the world as well as one of the oldest to formally adopt Christianity.
  • Zhuhai, Guangdong, China: Ninth-largest city of Guangdong and one of the earliest Special Economic Zones of China established in the late 1970s, specifically located north of the then-Portuguese territory of Macau.
