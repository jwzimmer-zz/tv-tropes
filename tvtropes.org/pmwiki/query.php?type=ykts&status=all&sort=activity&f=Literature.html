Japanese folk tale that had a woman throwing a sandal Literature

I saw this story as a children's picture book like 20 years ago so I'm not sure if it's an actual Japanese folk tale. Unfortunately, I don't remember any names.

Here are the details I remember:

  • Warlord rules over a poor village and levies heavy taxes on their harvest.
  • We first meet the heroine as a little girl living under the Warlord's rule.
  • When she's an adult, she's engaged to a man with an easy laugh and strong hands.
  • Her fiancé is also sort of a rebel leader who the Warlord has trouble dealing with.
    • I think his rebels were driving off the warlord's Evil Debt Collectors
  • Warlord, after consulting his advisor, decides to marry the heroine to demoralize the fiancé and his rebel peasants.
  • Warlord's soldiers take heroine away before her family can have their evening meal and bring her to the Warlord just as he's finishing his evening meal.
  • After Warlord proposes, heroine says she'll go outside and thank the gods for this great honor. Once she's out, she runs off to the nearest temple (or convent?) for sanctuary.
    • I think this temple was related to a Dragon god?
  • She reaches the temple gate but the pursuing soldiers almost catch up to her. She throws her sandal onto the roof of the temple, which is treated the same as entering the temple. The soldiers cannot touch her, but now she has to spend 2 years in the temple.
  • After hearing what has happened, the fiancé gathers his badly outnumbered band of peasants to take on the Warlord's army.
  • The rebels win and have the Warlord surrounded. However, the fiancé decrees it takes only one man to kill him and duels him one-on-one. He wins.
  • After 2 years, heroine and fiancé are reunited and live happily ever after.

Old book about a boy in the Cub Scouts Literature

True-to-life book for boys written in maybe the 1950s or 1960s; my parents bought used for me in the 1970s. I don't know that it was published by/on behalf of the Boy Scouts of America but it could have been.

It is about a boy whose family moves into a new town and he wants to join the Cub Scouts - the real Cub Scouts, not a Scout-Out situation - but he isn't allowed to because all the dens are full. If he can find enough boys to start a new den they could all join together. He does, and they do. His mother becomes their den mother (not den leader, as it was called by the time I was in Cub Scouts).

At the pack meeting where they join, held in the school gym, there's a tug-of-war activity after the meeting. Our heros win, even though they were all new and/or younger than the rest of the pack, because the rest were all wearing the full Cub Scout uniform including hard-soled shoes whereas the new kids were all wearing sneakers (except one boy whose mother always makes him dress up, and he is wearing shoes with crepe soles so they're practically as good as sneakers) so they have better traction on the gym floor.

At a later point in the book the boys in the new den (which is well established by now, but it's still their first year I think) happen upon a small house fire. and all the boys know what to do: run to the fire alarm call box and get the fire department. (Remember, this was in the 1950s before everyone had a cell phone.) We follow our protagonist as he does this. But it turns out that all the other boys did it too, and since they don't all live in the same neighborhood note Normally any given den is made up of boys from the same few block radius, but since their den was made for all the new boys they're from all over they all ran to their own neighborhoods' fire alarm call boxes - because that's where they knew where one was - so this relatively small house fire is suddenly a six-alarmer.
