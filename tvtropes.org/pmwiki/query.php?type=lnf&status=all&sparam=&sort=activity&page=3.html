Standard usage in non-standard forms

Something is used in a manner that is similar to its intended function, without being similar enough to be considered the same thing. For example; a VTOL aircraft may not have need for a runway, especially if it doesn't have any wheels, but when it's approaching the airport at full speed, flying over the runway as if it were coming in to land means it has a mile or so of flat, obstruction-free ground to decelerate safely, as opposed to a hard stop that might break a few things (ranging from structural components to FAA regulations).

"But I was nice to you!"

A character is on a Roaring Rampage of Revenge or similar tables-have-turned situation. Another character who was nice to them in the past (or at least less of a jerk to them) tries to bring this up in the hopes of being spared.

For instance, Alice was kidnapped by criminals for ransom, with one of them (Bob) doing what he can to make her comfortable like letting her watch TV or bringing her snacks (or in a darker version, is the only one not to make rape threats to shut her up). The cops arrive and arrest the criminals, and Bob is flabbergasted (or even feels betrayed) when she doesn't mention this at the trial.

"Stop doing X!" "Stop MAKING me do X!"

Alice asks Bob to stop doing something. Bob points out he's only doing it as a result of Alice's (or whatever group Alice is a part of) actions.

For example:

  • Alice (the invaded) wants Bob (the invader) out of her country, Bob responds that Alice's country broke the peace treaty first.
  • Alice tells Bob to stop throwing away the spinach and broccoli she puts in his lunch as it's a waste of money, he answers that he wouldn't have to throw them away if she'd stop putting them in there in the first place.
  • Bob is a filmmaker whose every work presents Alice's political party as ignorant, small-minded and hypocritical bigots. Alice asks that he provide a less straw-filled version in his next movie, Bob responds that he will as soon as they stop giving him cause to portray them as such.

Unqualified but surprisingly competent

Character somehow ends up getting a job that, in theory, they are woefully unqualified for. However, due to persistence, hidden talent, and good old fashioned common sense, they end up being really good at it. In some cases, they might turn out to be better at their jobs than other, theoretically better qualified characters.

Sometimes happens in prince-and-the-pauper type stories like Dave, Trading Places, or King Ralph, but other examples might include Tommy Boy, Critical Condition, or the MASH episode featuring an ordinary soldier who was pretending to be a surgeon (and was actually good at it).
